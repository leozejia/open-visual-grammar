# Big Character Poster Failure Signals

These are symptoms that the design language has gone weak. They are not a list
of forbidden objects.

## No Attitude

The image explains a topic but does not take a position.

It may look clean, balanced, and technically correct, but it does not feel like
someone is saying something in public.

## Generic Evidence

The image uses abstract blocks, glow, dashboards, or vague tech surfaces when
the article has more concrete pain available.

The fix is usually not "remove the object." The fix is to find the real
evidence object: a bill, receipt, command slip, local record, plugin surface,
handoff note, or other artifact that makes the pain specific.

## Decorative Metaphor

The metaphor sits near the title but does not change anything.

In this design language, objects should act. They weigh, tear, expose, bend,
stamp, stain, compress, or reveal.

## Template Safety

The image feels like it was optimized to avoid mistakes rather than to make a
strong cover.

Common symptoms:

- the typography feels pasted on;
- all risky evidence has been removed;
- the scene becomes a tasteful background;
- the prompt reads like a compliance checklist;
- the result could sell any AI article.

## Over-Explaining

The poster tries to include every lesson from the article.

A good poster usually compresses the piece into one public correction. Details
can appear as texture or evidence, but the first read should stay sharp.

## False Drama

The image creates a crisis larger than the article's real stakes.

Operational warnings can be hot, rough, and funny without becoming disaster
theater.

## Prompt Worship

The operator starts preserving old prompt sentences instead of preserving why
the image worked.

Historical prompts can be useful evidence, but the durable asset is the visual
judgment:

```text
article pain -> public correction -> concrete evidence -> visual action -> poster attitude
```
